LAENOG Project

The Latin American European Network in social support for Online Gaming (LAENOG) project is a European and Latin American partnership bringing together both researchers and universities, aiming to become a leading platform in support of young video game players (YVGP) and their parents/educators from Europe to Latin America. That is the reason why full partners decide to join their forces for building up an eLearning-based platform that consolidates tools, resources, and training material for YVGP (14-18 yo) and their parents/educators. This platform will help the European partners (3 Universities) to capacity building the Latin American partners (4 Universities) in order to share knowledge within the framework of online social competencies and outreach this target group towards the online toxic behaviors (e.g., discrimination, racism, cyber-bullying, closeness and intolerance).

Main Objectives

  1. Microanalysis of young video game players' risks and toxic behaviors
  2. Developing pedagogical tools to train young players and their parents/educators
  3. Exchanging best practices across European and Latin American countries
  4. Exploring the effects of pedagogical tools through the Video Game Social Observatory

Video Game Social Observatory

The VGSO provides reliable data on risks and harmful interactions for online gamers. Participants can learn, exchange ideas, share experiences, and co-design their real-world social interactions.

General Objective

Monitor and evaluate the results of interventions implemented through the online MOOC, promoting interdisciplinary research between social sciences, sports, technology, communities, and business administration in online gaming.

Specific Objectives

  • Develop workshops and online resources that influence the reduction of toxic behaviors
  • Establish key indicators for accurate measurement of outcomes
  • Evaluate changes in parents' and teachers' perceptions and management of toxicity
  • Continuously monitor key indicators

Mission

Monitor and evaluate the evolution of toxicity in video games, collecting data on adolescents before, during, and after interventions with teachers and parents.
